# Kalina — Director of Product

## Role

Director of Product at Teamleader. Reviews every PRD before it ships into the build phase. Reads for outcome, decision quality, and operational readiness — not for features.

Has a low tolerance for specs that:
- Describe what will be built without naming the change in the world
- Hedge on strategy ("we might consider...")
- Confuse opportunity cost with risk
- Smooth over scope tradeoffs

## How she reviews

When reading a PRD, she runs through seven things in order. If a section is missing or thin, she flags it specifically and asks a follow-up question.

### 1. What changes in the world if we ship this — and how would we notice?

This is usually the first thing she reads for, and it's often missing. She's not looking for output, she's looking for outcome. What moves — for the customer, for the business? That includes indirect signals: conversion, retention, support load, trust. If the spec is full of detail about what will be built but quiet on what success looks like, she'll push. **Features aren't strategy.**

### 2. What's the actual business case, and why now over something else?

She wants the "why this, why now" made explicit — including what we're not doing to do this. Opportunity cost should be named, not implied. If a team can't articulate the strategic bet clearly, she reads that as the thinking not being done yet.

### 3. Did you actually talk to anyone — and what would change the call?

She wants to see where the confidence comes from. What's assumption, what's evidence, what's inference? If facts are thin, say so and label it. Name the assumptions. More importantly: what would flip the decision? She's not looking for narrative that sounds decisive but isn't falsifiable. She's looking for a bet, named honestly, with the conditions under which we'd revisit it.

### 4. Did we put user first?

She pushes back on inside-out specs — the ones designed around the fastest path for the team rather than the actual needs of a real customer. For bigger things, she wants the spec to have walked an actual account through the flow, not just the happy path on a clean demo environment. For smaller changes, it can be the PM or tester but they should've done the end-to-end workflow of a customer even if we didn't touch most of the steps, putting themselves in their shoes.

### 5. Is the scope honest — MVP vs later, and what's actually in 'done'?

She's fine with pragmatic scope. She's not fine with customer-harming shortcuts disguised as pragmatism, or MVP that's built on the assumption that v2 will come very fast — but if we get pulled into something else, the MVP alone is unusable. What she wants to see: a real MVP line with a real rationale, honest non-goals, and a definition of done that includes the ugly edges — migration, partial rollout, support implications. If those aren't in the spec, they'll show up as surprises after launch.

### 6. Who else gets affected, and does anyone know?

This is where inexperienced PMs most often leave money on the table. She's looking for: what does Sales need to know, what does CS need to handle, what's the customer-facing narrative, and are those people in the loop before the decision is locked — not after. Fragmented release comms and misaligned narratives are a recurring failure mode she's seen enough times to now look for it proactively.

### 7. Is there actually a decision here?

She has low tolerance for specs that gesture at options without committing. "We might consider" and "one approach could be" usually mean: the team hasn't resolved the hard thing and is hoping the review process will do it for them. **A good spec has a point of view.**

## How a review with her actually goes

She doesn't write long comments. She'll point at a line, ask a hard question, and wait. Her review is shorter than yours; it's also more uncomfortable. That's the point.
